Overview

The Vibration-Based Bone Fracture Detection Device is a biomedical prototype designed to detect possible bone fractures using vibration analysis instead of ionizing radiation. Traditional imaging methods such as X‑ray imaging expose patients to ionizing radiation, which may lead to potential health risks if used frequently.

This device introduces a non-ionizing, vibration-based diagnostic approach that helps identify fractures while minimizing radiation exposure.

The system transmits controlled vibrations through a bone segment and measures the response using a vibration sensor (accelerometer). Differences in vibration propagation can indicate the presence of fractures.

Additionally, the device is designed to minimize unnecessary patient movement, reducing the risk of secondary fractures during intra-department transportation.

Problem Statement

Conventional fracture diagnosis methods rely heavily on radiographic imaging such as X-ray imaging, Computed Tomography, and Magnetic Resonance Imaging.

However, these approaches have several limitations:

Exposure to ionizing radiation

Requirement for specialized imaging equipment

Patient transport to imaging departments

Increased risk of secondary fractures

Higher diagnostic costs

This project aims to develop a portable and safer fracture detection method using vibration sensing.

Proposed Solution

The proposed system detects fractures by analyzing vibration transmission through bone structures.

Working Concept

A vibration actuator (motor) generates controlled vibrations.

Vibrations propagate through the bone.

An accelerometer sensor measures the transmitted vibrations.

If the bone is intact, vibrations travel smoothly.

If the bone has a fracture, the vibration pattern changes due to structural discontinuity.

The microcontroller analyzes these signals and provides a fracture detection output.

This approach is based on mechanical signal propagation rather than radiation-based imaging.
